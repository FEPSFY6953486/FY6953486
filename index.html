Introduction

Aviation contributes approximately 2–3% of global carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions and is responsible for additional non-CO₂ climate effects such as contrails and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) (Lee et al., 2023). Sustainable Aviation Fuels (SAFs) are drop-in hydrocarbon fuels produced from renewable or waste-based feedstocks. These are considered the most immediate option to reduce aviation’s lifecycle greenhouse gas (GHG) footprint while maintaining compatibility with existing engines and infrastructure (Yoo et al., 2022). However, SAFs differ substantially from traditional petroleum-based jet fuels in production methods, environmental performance, scalability, and cost. This paper compares SAF and conventional jet fuel across production pathways, emissions, flight performance, economics and environmental benefits to evaluate their role in decarbonizing aviation.

Analysis and Discussion

Production Pathways and Feedstocks

Traditional Jet-A fuel is derived from crude oil through fractional distillation and hydroprocessing. SAFs have a much more complex production method and are produced via several certified pathways under ASTM D7566, including Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids (HEFA), Fischer–Tropsch Synthetic Paraffinic Kerosene (FT-SPK), Alcohol-to-Jet (ATJ), and emerging Power-to-Liquid (PtL) or electrofuel routes (Jing et al., 2022). HEFA fuels are produced from waste cooking oil, animal fats, or other lipids. HEFA currently the most commercialized amongst businesses. FT-SPK fuels are derived from gasified biomass or waste gases and PtL routes synthesize hydrocarbons using captured CO₂ and green hydrogen, however this is a more complex production line compared to HEFA (Yilmaz et al., 2017). The environmental performance and cost of SAFs depend heavily on feedstock type, process energy source and regional conditions (Zahid et al., 2024).

Lifecycle Greenhouse Gas Emissions

The key advantage of SAFs is their potential to significantly reduce lifecycle (well-to-wake) GHG emissions compared with fossil jet fuel. HEFA fuels made from waste lipids typically achieve 50–80% emission reductions. However, FT-SPK fuels from municipal solid waste or forestry residues can yield similar or greater benefits depending on production (Yoo et al., 2022). Electrofuels (PtL or e-SAF) offer near-zero emissions if powered by renewable electricity and if captured CO₂ originates from atmospheric or biogenic sources (Yilmaz et al., 2017). Conversely, crop-based SAFs such as those from palm or soy oil can have limited or even negative benefits if indirect land-use change emissions are high (Zahid et al., 2024). For context, the baseline GHG intensity of petroleum jet fuel varies between 85 and 95 gCO₂e/MJ depending on crude source and refining efficiency (Jing et al., 2022). Therefore, a credible SAF comparison must specify the fossil baseline and life-cycle boundaries.

Flight Performance and Certification

SAFs are designed as “drop-in” fuels. "Drop-in" means they can directly replace or blend with Jet-A fuel without engine modifications. This can save a large amount of money and save a lot of time aswell. Under ASTM D7566, SAFs can currently be blended up to 50% with conventional fuel but, some pathways (e.g., synthesized iso-paraffins) are being tested for 100% use without the need of Jet-A Fuel (Calderon et al., 2024). Flight trials have demonstrated that SAFs provide equivalent energy content and combustion properties while reducing particulate matter and aromatic content in the atmosphere. This may also decrease contrail formation and non-CO₂ climate impacts (Moore et al., 2023). Operational data confirm that aircraft performance and maintenance are unaffected when certified fuels are used.

Economics and Scalability

Despite environmental benefits and net-zero goals, SAF production costs remain significantly higher than for fossil jet fuel. Current HEFA production costs range from USD 1.00–2.50 per litre, compared to roughly USD 0.50 for conventional Jet-A (Chireshe et al., 2025). PtL fuels are even more expensive, mainly due to the high cost of renewable electricity and electrolytic hydrogen needed to produce the fuel (Chireshe et al., 2024). Feedstock availability presents another limitation: global supplies of used cooking oil and animal fats are finite and already in high demand for road biofuels which are used to land vehicles. Large-scale decarbonization will therefore require a transition to lignocellulosic residues (these are non-food plant-based waste materials from sources like agriculture (straw, bagasse), forestry (sawdust), and the pulp and paper industry), waste gases, or synthetic fuels from captured CO₂ (Yoo et al., 2022). Policy mechanisms such as blending mandates, production tax credits, and carbon pricing are emerging to narrow the cost gap. The EU’s ReFuelEU Aviation initiative and the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act’s SAF tax credits are early examples of such measures (ICAO, 2024).

Environmental Co-benefits and Sustainability

Beyond CO₂, SAFs can reduce non-CO₂ effects through lower soot and sulfur emissions. These emissions are also large by-products that affect the atmosphere. Reduced aromatic content decreases contrail formation potential which offers additional climate benefits (Moore et al., 2023). Nevertheless, sustainability extends beyond carbon metrics and can affect different areas such as: crop-based fuels may impact food security, biodiversity, and water use if poorly managed. The Roundtable on Sustainable Biomaterials (RSB) is crucial to ensure that SAF deployment aligns with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (ICAO, 2024).

Conclusion

To Conclude, Sustainable Aviation Fuels represent the most feasible short to mid-term pathway to decarbonize aviation while maintaining compatibility with existing infrastructure. Waste and residue-based SAFs already achieve 50–80% lifecycle GHG reductions, while electrofuels could deliver near-zero emissions as renewable power becomes more accessible. However, the cost competitiveness, limited feedstock availability and sustainability governance remain major obstacles for the aviation sector to meet this net-zero goal. Strong policy support, targeted subsidies and clear sustainability criteria are needed to expand production while avoiding unintended environmental or social trade-offs. In the longer term, SAFs will likely coexist with emerging technologies such as hydrogen and electric aircraft. Their rapid deployment over the next two decades is critical to meeting the aviation industry’s net-zero 2050 goals.
