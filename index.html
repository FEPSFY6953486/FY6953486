Introduction

Aviation contributes approximately 2–3% of global carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions and is responsible for additional non-CO₂ climate effects such as contrails and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) (Lee et al., 2021). Sustainable Aviation Fuels (SAFs)—drop-in hydrocarbon fuels produced from renewable or waste-based feedstocks—are considered the most immediate option to reduce aviation’s lifecycle greenhouse gas (GHG) footprint while maintaining compatibility with existing engines and infrastructure (Yoo et al., 2022). However, SAFs differ substantially from traditional petroleum-based jet fuels in production methods, environmental performance, scalability, and cost. This paper compares SAF and conventional jet fuel across production pathways, emissions, flight performance, economics, and policy context to evaluate their role in decarbonizing aviation.

Analysis and Discussion

Production Pathways and Feedstocks

Traditional Jet-A fuel is derived from crude oil through fractional distillation and hydroprocessing. SAFs, by contrast, are produced via several certified pathways under ASTM D7566, including Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids (HEFA), Fischer–Tropsch Synthetic Paraffinic Kerosene (FT-SPK), Alcohol-to-Jet (ATJ), and emerging Power-to-Liquid (PtL) or electrofuel routes (Jing et al., 2022). HEFA fuels—produced from waste cooking oil, animal fats, or other lipids—are currently the most commercialized. FT-SPK fuels are derived from gasified biomass or waste gases, while PtL routes synthesize hydrocarbons using captured CO₂ and green hydrogen (Ozkan et al., 2024). The environmental performance and cost of SAFs depend heavily on feedstock type, process energy source, and regional conditions (Zahid, 2024).

Lifecycle Greenhouse Gas Emissions

The key advantage of SAFs is their potential to significantly reduce lifecycle (well-to-wake) GHG emissions compared with fossil jet fuel. HEFA fuels made from waste lipids typically achieve 50–80% emission reductions, while FT-SPK fuels from municipal solid waste or forestry residues can yield similar or greater benefits (Yoo et al., 2022). Electrofuels (PtL or e-SAF) offer near-zero emissions if powered by renewable electricity and if captured CO₂ originates from atmospheric or biogenic sources (Ozkan et al., 2024). Conversely, crop-based SAFs such as those from palm or soy oil can have limited or even negative benefits if indirect land-use change emissions are high (Zahid, 2024). For context, the baseline GHG intensity of petroleum jet fuel varies between 85 and 95 gCO₂e/MJ depending on crude source and refining efficiency (Jing et al., 2022). Thus, a credible SAF comparison must specify the fossil baseline and life-cycle boundaries.

Flight Performance and Certification

SAFs are designed as “drop-in” fuels, meaning they can directly replace or blend with Jet-A without engine modifications. Under ASTM D7566, SAFs can currently be blended up to 50% with conventional fuel, though some pathways (e.g., synthesized iso-paraffins) are being tested for 100% use (NREL, 2024). Flight trials have demonstrated that SAFs provide equivalent energy content and combustion properties while reducing particulate matter and aromatic content, which may also decrease contrail formation and non-CO₂ climate impacts (Moore et al., 2023). Operational data confirm that aircraft performance and maintenance are unaffected when certified fuels are used.

Economics and Scalability

Despite environmental benefits, SAF production costs remain significantly higher than for fossil jet fuel. Current HEFA production costs range from USD 1.00–2.50 per litre, compared to roughly USD 0.50 for conventional Jet-A (ICCT, 2023). PtL fuels are even more expensive, mainly due to the high cost of renewable electricity and electrolytic hydrogen (Ozkan et al., 2024). Feedstock availability presents another limitation: global supplies of used cooking oil and animal fats are finite and already in high demand for road biofuels. Large-scale decarbonization will therefore require a transition to lignocellulosic residues, waste gases, or synthetic fuels from captured CO₂ (Yoo et al., 2022). Policy mechanisms such as blending mandates, production tax credits, and carbon pricing are emerging to narrow the cost gap. The EU’s ReFuelEU Aviation initiative and the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act’s SAF tax credits are early examples of such measures (ICAO, 2024).

Environmental Co-benefits and Sustainability

Beyond CO₂, SAFs can reduce non-CO₂ effects through lower soot and sulfur emissions. Reduced aromatic content decreases contrail formation potential, offering additional climate benefits (Moore et al., 2023). Nevertheless, sustainability extends beyond carbon metrics: crop-based fuels may impact food security, biodiversity, and water use if poorly managed (Zahid, 2024). Comprehensive sustainability certification—such as through the Roundtable on Sustainable Biomaterials (RSB)—is crucial to ensure that SAF deployment aligns with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (ICAO, 2024).

Policy and Future Prospects

Government intervention remains essential to expand SAF production and adoption. International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) mechanisms like CORSIA recognize SAFs that meet specific lifecycle GHG thresholds, enabling emission offset credits (ICAO, 2024). However, projections indicate that current global SAF capacity—estimated at less than 1% of total jet fuel demand—will fall far short of 2030 targets without major investment (NREL, 2024). Coordinated policy frameworks that support both HEFA scale-up and long-term PtL development are therefore vital. Research and innovation also focus on improving process yields, scaling feedstock collection, and integrating carbon capture to achieve carbon-negative fuels. The combination of SAFs, efficiency improvements, and potentially hydrogen-electric propulsion represents a realistic multi-pronged path to sustainable aviation (Lee et al., 2021).
